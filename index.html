Machine Learning vs. Deep Learning

A Comparative Analysis of Approaches — AIML 500 Coursework

Introduction

As part of the AIML 500 coursework, this written analysis explores a foundational question in applied machine intelligence: when should you use traditional machine learning, and when is deep learning the right choice? Through two real-world examples email spam detection and machine translation — this artifact examines how problem structure dictates the most appropriate modelling approach.

Description

This artifact presents a comparative analysis of machine learning and deep learning, grounded in two concrete industry examples. The first is email spam detection, a binary classification problem well-served by traditional algorithms. The second is machine translation (as used in Google Translate), a complex generative task that demands the contextual depth of deep learning architectures. Together, these examples illustrate the principle that the best model is not necessarily the most complex one — it is the one best matched to the nature and structure of the problem.

Objective

The objective of this artifact is to demonstrate critical thinking about AI model selection and to apply course concepts to practical scenarios. Specifically, the goals were to: compare the capabilities and limitations of traditional ML algorithms versus deep learning architectures; analyse two distinct real-world use cases and justify the appropriateness of each technique; and articulate the principle that effective AI engineering requires matching model complexity to problem complexity — not defaulting to the most advanced tool available.

Analysis & Key Findings

Why ML Works for Spam

  • Spam detection is fundamentally a binary classification task — structured and well-defined.
  • Features such as word frequency and sender metadata are easy to extract and interpret.
  • Algorithms like Naive Bayes are efficient, interpretable, and fast to retrain as patterns evolve.
  • Deep learning would over-engineer the problem, adding cost and complexity without meaningful benefit.

Why Deep Learning Wins for Translation

  • Human language is sequential and context-dependent — the meaning of a word shifts with surrounding words.
  • Transformer attention mechanisms capture long-range dependencies across full sentences.
  • Traditional ML cannot handle the generative nature of translation — producing new sentences, not just classifying input.
  • Deep learning is the only viable approach when structural complexity of data exceeds what fixed features can represent.

Conclusion & Value Proposition
These two examples highlight a foundational principle in applied machine learning: the best approach depends on the nature of the problem. When the data is structured and the task involves straightforward classification, simpler models such as Naive Bayes or Random Forest are often more efficient and practical. However, when dealing with complex, unstructured data like human language, deep learning becomes necessary because it can learn patterns and relationships that simpler models cannot capture. Choosing the right technique is not about always using the most advanced model available — effective machine learning involves matching the complexity of the model to the complexity of the problem.

Neural Networks Explained Simply

A Plain-Language Guide to How Machines Learn — AIML 500 Coursework

Introduction

Neural networks are the engine behind some of the most powerful AI systems in use today — from the voice assistant on your phone to the models that detect cancer in medical scans. Yet the concept itself is more intuitive than it might first appear. This artifact provides a clear, jargon-free explanation of what neural networks are, how they work, why they learn, and where they are applied — grounded in everyday analogies that make the ideas stick.

The Core Idea — What Is a Neural Network?

A neural network is a system that learns from examples. You show it thousands of labelled inputs — pictures of cats and dogs, emails marked spam or not, audio clips with their transcriptions — and it gradually adjusts itself until it can make accurate predictions on data it has never seen before. It does not follow hand-written rules. It discovers its own patterns through repeated exposure and correction.

The name comes from the human brain. Biological neurons are cells that fire electrical signals to one another. A neural network mimics this loosely: it is made up of many simple computational units — also called neurons or nodes — connected together in layers, each passing information forward and adjusting the strength of those connections based on how well it is doing.

The Best Analogy — Learning to Recognise Handwriting
✏️
Imagine you are learning to grade handwritten numbers

At first you have no idea. Someone shows you a "7" and tells you it is a seven. You notice some features: it has an angled stroke, a horizontal bar at the top, no loops. You see a thousand more examples — some sloppy, some neat, some rotated — and each time you are told whether you were right or wrong. Over time, your internal sense of "what makes a 7 a 7" sharpens. A neural network does exactly this, except instead of a human adjusting their intuition, the network adjusts millions of numerical weights — tiny dials that control how strongly one neuron influences the next — until its predictions match the correct answers as closely as possible.

The Three Layers — Input, Hidden, Output

Every neural network is organised into layers. Data enters at the left, passes through one or more hidden layers where the actual learning happens, and exits as a prediction at the right.

Layer 1
Input Layer

Receives the raw data. For an image, each neuron in this layer represents one pixel. For a loan application, each neuron might represent one field — age, income, credit score. No learning happens here; this layer simply passes the data in.

Layer 2  (one or many)
Hidden Layer(s)

This is where the intelligence lives. Each neuron combines signals from the previous layer, applies a weight to each one, sums them up, and decides how much signal to pass forward. Deep networks stack many hidden layers — each one learning more abstract features than the last: edges, then shapes, then objects.

Layer 3
Output Layer

Produces the final answer. For image classification it might output probabilities — "92% cat, 8% dog." For a translation task it outputs a sequence of words. For fraud detection it outputs a single number: the probability of fraud.

Simplified neural network — data flows left to right through layers of weighted connections
How the Network Actually Learns

Learning in a neural network comes down to three steps repeated thousands of times:

Forward Pass — Make a Guess

  • Data enters the input layer and flows forward through every hidden layer.
  • Each neuron multiplies its inputs by weights, sums the results, and applies an activation function that decides how much signal passes forward.
  • The output layer produces a prediction — for example, "this email is spam."

Backward Pass — Learn from Mistakes

  • The network compares its prediction to the correct answer and calculates the error — called the loss.
  • A process called backpropagation traces the error back through every layer, calculating how much each weight contributed to the mistake.
  • An optimiser (commonly gradient descent) nudges every weight slightly in the direction that reduces the error. This cycle repeats until accuracy is high enough.
Key Concepts — Plain-Language Glossary
Weight

A number that controls how strongly one neuron influences the next. Learning means adjusting weights until predictions improve.

Activation Function

A gate that decides how much signal a neuron passes forward. Common ones (ReLU, sigmoid) introduce the non-linearity that lets networks learn complex patterns.

Loss Function

A score that measures how wrong the network's predictions are. Lower is better. Training is the process of minimising this score.

Backpropagation

The algorithm that works backwards through the network after each prediction, calculating how much each weight should change to reduce the error.

Gradient Descent

The optimisation strategy: like rolling a ball down a hill to find the lowest point, it nudges weights in the direction that reduces loss the fastest.

Epoch

One full pass through the entire training dataset. Networks typically train for many epochs before reaching acceptable accuracy.

Overfitting

When a network memorises training data instead of learning general patterns — like a student who memorises answers without understanding the subject.

Deep Learning

Neural networks with many hidden layers. "Deep" refers to the depth of the network. More layers allow the model to learn increasingly abstract representations.

Conclusion
A neural network is, at its core, a pattern-finding machine. It takes in data, makes a guess, measures how wrong it was, adjusts its internal dials, and tries again — millions of times — until it becomes reliably accurate. The layers give it the ability to go from raw pixels or words to high-level understanding. The weights encode everything the network has learned. Backpropagation is how it corrects itself. And activation functions give it the flexibility to learn patterns that no simple formula could describe. Understanding these fundamentals does not require a mathematics degree — it requires only the right analogy, applied patiently. Once the mental model clicks, the rest of modern AI becomes far less mysterious.

Data Challenges in Machine Learning

Training, Deployment, and the Hidden Complexity of Real-World Data — AIML 500 Coursework

Introduction

Building a machine learning model is often described as a technical triumph — choosing the right algorithm, tuning hyperparameters, achieving high accuracy on a benchmark. But in production environments, the largest and most consequential obstacles rarely live in the model itself. They live in the data. This artifact examines the landscape of data challenges encountered when training and deploying ML applications — from structural imperfections like imbalance and overlap, to operational concerns like privacy, drift, and scalability — and draws connections to the real systems a practising enterprise developer encounters every day.

Description

This artifact provides a structured analysis of the key data challenges that affect machine learning across its full lifecycle — from initial data acquisition through model training to live deployment. It categorises these challenges by their nature (structural, operational, ethical, and temporal), examines their real-world impact, and explores mitigation strategies employed in modern ML engineering. The analysis draws on published research in data-centric AI, industry best practices, and direct parallels to enterprise banking and insurance systems.

Objective

The objective of this artifact is to develop a critical, practitioner-level understanding of why data — not models — is most often the limiting factor in deployed ML systems. Specifically, the goals were to: catalogue and explain the full spectrum of data challenges with concrete examples; demonstrate how each challenge impacts model accuracy, fairness, and reliability; connect data quality concepts to real production scenarios in regulated industries such as banking and insurance; and articulate how the emerging paradigm of Data-Centric AI addresses these challenges differently from traditional model-centric approaches.

The Foundational Principle
🗑️
"Garbage in, garbage out" — the curse of learning from data

Machine learning algorithms are designed to learn from training data and expect that data to be well-behaved across several properties: equally represented classes, consistent feature values, correctly labelled instances, and stable distributions over time. In practice, these assumptions are broken far more often than they are met. The gap between data and quality data is where most ML projects quietly fail — not in the architecture of the model, but in the imperfections of the data it was fed.

Taxonomy of Data Imperfections

Research in data-centric AI identifies a consistent set of data imperfections that arise across real-world domains — not always due to errors in collection, but often as a natural product of the domain itself. When left unaddressed, each one biases or degrades model behaviour.

Imbalanced Data Underrepresented Sub-Concepts Class Overlap Small or Sparse Data Inconsistent Data Irrelevant Features Redundant Data Noisy Data Dataset Shift / Drift Missing Data
Core Data Challenges — Training Phase
⚖️
Data Availability

Sufficient, relevant data can be difficult to obtain — especially in niche domains, regulated industries, or novel applications. Models trained on insufficient data fail to generalise and overfit to the patterns they have seen.

High Impact
🔍
Data Quality

Incomplete records, inaccurate values, inconsistent formatting, and noise in raw data corrupt training signals. A model trained on dirty data learns patterns that do not exist in the real world — or misses ones that do.

High Impact
⚖️
Imbalanced Classes

When one class vastly outnumbers another — fraud transactions versus legitimate ones, rare disease versus healthy — models default to predicting the majority class. The minority class, often the one that matters most, gets systematically ignored.

High Impact
📋
Data Labelling

Supervised learning requires labelled data, but labelling at scale is expensive, slow, and prone to human error. Crowd-sourced annotations introduce inconsistency; domain expert labelling is costly. Label quality directly caps model accuracy.

High Impact
📊
Underrepresented Sub-Concepts

Within a class, small clusters of rare but valid examples — called small disjuncts — are frequently missed or overfit. In healthcare, rare disease subtypes or atypical presentations fall into this category, leading to dangerous misclassifications.

Medium Impact
🎯
Class Overlap

When instances of different classes occupy the same region of the feature space, models struggle to draw clean decision boundaries. Overlap is particularly common in fraud detection, where normal and fraudulent transactions share many surface features.

Medium Impact
Operational Data Challenges — Deployment Phase
🔒
Data Privacy & Security

Personal and sensitive data used in ML — transaction histories, medical records, insurance claims — must comply with regulations such as GDPR and HIPAA. Privacy constraints limit data access, complicate storage, and restrict the ability to share datasets across teams or organisations.

High Impact
⚖
Data Bias

Historical inequalities embedded in training data propagate into model predictions. A loan approval model trained on decades of biased lending decisions will encode and perpetuate those patterns. In regulated banking and insurance, biased models carry legal, regulatory, and reputational risk.

High Impact
🔄
Data Drift

The real world changes. Consumer behaviour shifts, fraud techniques evolve, economic conditions change — and the distribution of incoming data gradually diverges from the training data. Without monitoring and retraining, models silently degrade as the world moves on without them.

High Impact
💾
Data Scalability

Processing and storing massive datasets for training requires significant infrastructure — distributed systems, cloud storage, GPU clusters. Scaling data pipelines introduces complexity, cost, and new failure modes that must be engineered and monitored.

Medium Impact
⏱️
Real-Time Data Processing

Applications like payment fraud detection, algorithmic trading, and autonomous systems require inference on live streaming data — not static batches. Models trained on historical snapshots must adapt to real-time inputs without the latency of traditional batch processing pipelines.

Medium Impact
🔗
Data Integration

Enterprise ML models commonly draw from multiple heterogeneous sources — structured databases, unstructured documents, real-time event streams, third-party APIs. Reconciling these formats, schemas, and update frequencies requires robust data engineering before any model training can begin.

Medium Impact
Impact Severity Framework

Not all data challenges carry equal weight. The consequences range from degraded accuracy to catastrophic ethical failures. This framework categorises challenges by their potential impact on model reliability and downstream outcomes.

Critical Model Failure Bias, severe imbalance, data drift — model behaves incorrectly or unethically in production
Significant Degraded Accuracy Class overlap, label noise, missing data — model performs below acceptable thresholds
Operational Engineering Cost Scalability, versioning, integration — model development slows or becomes more expensive
Relevance to Banking & Insurance Systems

Banking Applications

  • Fraud detection models face extreme class imbalance — fraudulent transactions may be fewer than 0.1% of all activity, yet must be caught accurately.
  • Customer data used for credit scoring is governed by strict regulatory frameworks, limiting what features can be used and requiring model explainability.
  • Payment behaviour shifts over time with economic cycles, requiring continuous monitoring for data drift and model retraining cadences.
  • Multi-source integration — core banking, card systems, external credit bureaus — creates persistent data consistency and interoperability challenges.

Insurance Applications

  • Claims processing models trained on historical claims inherit any historical biases in approval and settlement patterns.
  • Rare claim types and unusual injury patterns represent small disjuncts — minority sub-concepts that models fail to learn from insufficient examples.
  • NCCI workers' compensation data integrations (as implemented in Sapiens experience) require careful versioning and reconciliation across data releases.
  • Batch processing systems using Spring Batch must handle data quality issues — missing fields, duplicate records — before feeding data to downstream models.

The Paradigm Shift — From Model-Centric to Data-Centric AI
🔄
The field is moving from optimising models to systematically improving data

The past decades of AI research focused on producing better architectures — a paradigm known as Model-Centric AI. Current research and industry practice is shifting toward a different approach: identifying and mitigating data quality issues systematically before and during model training, rather than attempting to compensate for them with model complexity. This Data-Centric AI paradigm involves a continuous cycle of data iteration — moving from imperfect to smart and actionable data — and requires practitioners to deeply understand the imperfections in their datasets, not just the architecture of their models. For enterprise developers integrating ML into production systems, this shift is directly actionable: investing in data pipelines, validation frameworks, and monitoring infrastructure often yields more reliable outcomes than tuning another hyperparameter.

Mitigation Strategies

Structural Data Issues

  • Imbalance: Apply oversampling techniques (SMOTE), undersampling, or class-weighted loss functions to give minority classes appropriate influence during training.
  • Missing Data: Use imputation strategies appropriate to the domain; document missingness patterns as they may themselves carry signal.
  • Noisy Labels: Invest in label quality review processes; use confident learning or label-smoothing techniques to reduce the impact of annotation errors.
  • Class Overlap: Apply dimensionality reduction, feature engineering, or ensemble methods that build separate classifiers for overlapping regions.

Operational & Temporal Issues

  • Data Drift: Implement statistical monitoring (PSI, KL divergence) on production data distributions; define retraining triggers when drift exceeds thresholds.
  • Privacy: Apply differential privacy, federated learning, or data anonymisation where direct access to raw PII is restricted by regulation.
  • Scalability: Design data pipelines with distributed processing frameworks (Spark, Kafka) and separate training infrastructure from inference serving.
  • Versioning: Treat datasets as versioned artefacts alongside model code; use tools like DVC or Delta Lake to maintain reproducibility across training runs.
Value Proposition & Unique Value
💡 Value Proposition

Data challenges are the primary reason ML projects fail in production — yet they receive far less attention than model selection. This analysis equips practitioners and stakeholders with a framework to anticipate, diagnose, and address data problems before they silently undermine deployed systems.

⭐ Unique Value

Written from the perspective of a developer with hands-on experience in banking and insurance data systems, this analysis connects abstract ML theory to the Spring Batch pipelines, NCCI integrations, and enterprise data environments where these challenges manifest every day in production.

Conclusion
The success of a machine learning system depends far less on the sophistication of its model than on the quality of the data it was trained on. Imbalanced classes cause models to ignore the events that matter most. Data drift causes models to silently degrade. Bias in training data becomes bias in decisions that affect real people. And poor data quality renders even the most powerful architecture unreliable. Recognising and addressing these challenges is not a preliminary step before the "real" ML work begins — it is the real ML work. The most robust path to a trustworthy deployed model is not a better algorithm — it is better data, systematically understood and continuously maintained.

Analysis & Key Findings

Cross-Industry Pattern

  • AI has moved from pilot projects to core infrastructure across all three sectors simultaneously in 2026.
  • In healthcare, ambient AI is giving clinicians time back while improving patient outcomes at scale.
  • In finance, agentic AI is executing multi-step workflows in real time while humans focus on strategy and oversight.
  • In retail, AI is compressing timelines, reducing waste, and enabling personalization at massive scale.

The Differentiating Question

  • The companies succeeding are not those who adopted AI fastest but those who asked the right question.
  • That question: which specific, painful problem does AI solve better than anything else available?
  • For Abridge: physician documentation burden. For Oracle: manual banking workflows. For Walmart: slow trend-to-shelf cycles.
  • Each chose a problem with measurable outcomes, deployed deliberately, and let results speak.
Future Potential by Sector
HC Healthcare: Abridge is expanding from passive transcription to active clinical reasoning via its Contextual Reasoning Engine, pulling from full patient history, prior visits, insurer guidelines, and peer-reviewed literature in real time.
